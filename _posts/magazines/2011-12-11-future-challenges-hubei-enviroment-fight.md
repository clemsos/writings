When you visit Wuhan for the first time, I bet you will be impressed by
the wide Yangzi river and its crazy traffic of boats carrying sand and
coal up to middle China through the [Three
Gorges](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Gorges). Located in Hubei
province, Wuhan is a huge conurbation composed of 3 cities (Wuchang,
Hankou and Hanyang) and delimited by two of the main rivers in China:
the Yangzi and the Han.

Wuhan has been famous for centuries for its large number of lakes, and
especially its Eastern Lake that *"covers 33 square km"* and hosted
*"the summer villa of Chairman Mao Zedong"* as [the
guidebook](http://www.travelchinaguide.com/attraction/hubei/wuhan/donghu.htm)
says. Hubei -- which literally means  "*at the North of the Lakes"* --
is *the* place for water in China, while China's heart beats in Wuhan.

**Citizen mobilization to protect the East Lake** {#citizen-mobilization-to-protect-the-east-lake style="text-align: justify;"}
-------------------------------------------------

But the East Lake is now famous for its [dead
fish](http://www.guardian.co.uk/news/gallery/2007/jul/18/china.pollution?picture=330216042).
If Yu Zhengsheng, the mayor of Wuhan until 2010, always fought the idea
of building on the lake, the current local administration has new
projects in mind. In spring 2010, they gave authorization to [OCT
Holding](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shenzhen_Overseas_Chinese_Town_Holding_Company)
to build a huge amusement park on this time-hallowed scenic spot of
great natural beauty. A few months later and without any previous
warning, a series of [violent
evictions](http://chinastudygroup.net/2010/04/the-battle-for-east-lake-in-wuhan/)
begun with the local mafia kicking all the fishermen and farmers off
their land. Within 3 weeks, the bulldozer arrived and started to tear
down houses, filling parts of the lake with rubble.

Confronted with this urgent situation, people in the neighborhood
decided on some protest actions. After a few attempts to write petitions
that ended in the police raiding the petitioners' houses, the idea of a
big artistic mobilization was born. First named *"Our East Lake"*, then
*"Every One's East Lake* ", the movement  brought together  artists,
researchers, architects, students and strollers over a period of several
months. They started a raft of projects to protect, document and
underscore the importance of such a place in Wuhan where pollution and
litter are ugly and all too common sights.

A [website](http://donghu2010.org/) was launched, featuring all
pictures, writing, shows, debates, talks, performances and drawings that
had been done (shown in the gallery below). *[The Every One's East
Lake](http://donghu2010.org)* initiative has greatly raised awarness of
the common fate of the inhabitants and brought great celebration to the
lake banks. But even if this local issue [has now
spread](http://www.infzm.com/content/43317) to the national level, the
amusement park project still continues apace and along with it the
wanton destruction of one of China's most beautiful  natural heritage
sites.

**The Han River: Selling water to the North** {#the-han-river-selling-water-to-the-north style="text-align: justify;"}
---------------------------------------------

In this region of wetlands, local and international businesses have
always been thirsty for more and more water. The Yangtze's longest
branch, [the Han
river,](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_River_(Hanshui)) flows from
[Shaanxi](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaanxi) to Wuhan covering a
distance of more than 1500 kms. It has been recently included in
the colossal China [South-North water
project](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South%E2%80%93North_Water_Transfer_Project)
that plans to divert the waters of the Yangzi to meet the needs of dry
Northern China. Indeed, Northern China is now facing desertification on
an unprecendently rapid scale, and Beijing water is already [10 times
under the UN scarcity
level](http://www.nytimes.com/2011/06/02/world/asia/02water.html?pagewanted=all).
Cities in the north can no longer sustain the water supply for their
growing populations and many rivers have been diverted in an attempt to
solve this crisis. China's neighbors, India, Pakistan and Laos, have
already [warned  the
government](http://www.dnaindia.com/analysis/comment_will-chinas-designs-on-the-brahmaputra-leave-india-parched_1569128)
about such practices that create [catastrophic results
downstream](http://www.sinlung.com/2011/04/china-dams-threaten-livelihoods.html).

**Yun Jianli and the Green Hanjiang Association: Determination to save the river** {#yun-jianli-and-the-green-hanjiang-association-determination-to-save-the-river style="text-align: justify;"}
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------

On a local level near Wuhan, the impact on the Han River is just as bad.
The quality of the water has degraded fast in measure with its
[increasing
scarcity](http://www.chinadialogue.net/article/show/single/en/4085)along
the river. In view of the great loss happening before their eyes, some
villagers and scholars from
[Xiangfan](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiangyang) have come together to
create an association called the [Green Han
River](http://www.greenhj.org.cn/do/bencandy.php?fid=49&id=1852) to
protect this priceless resource. The background of the founder Yun
Jianli speaks for itself: after retirement, she decided to dedicate
herself to the protection of the Han River. Using her previous
experience as a teacher and [CCP
me](http://www.spiegel.de/international/spiegel/0,1518,435188,00.html)[mber](http://www.spiegel.de/international/spiegel/0,1518,435188,00.html),
she traveled by bicycle through the district of Xiangfan looking for
office space and asking for help. After visiting villages and factories,
she started sending a welter of reports to local government authorities
to increase public awareness and support. Here is some short video
footage from ATV WORLD that features Jianli on a field trip near
Xiangfan (her home) :

Nominated in 2009 in the [1000 Women for
Peace](http://www.1000peacewomen.org/eng/friedensfrauen_biographien_gefunden.php?WomenID=1557),
 Jianli has now placed the Green Han River on the international stage.
It has been featured in various types of media, such as in this [PBS
documentary](http://www.pbs.org/kqed/chinainside/nature/activists.html)
which is well worth
[watching](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ko0CTml4AXs). Now the
association represents [Water Keeper](http://www.waterkeeper.org/) in
China, and Yun Jianli and her team  have been invited on [an official
trip to
France](http://www.ambafrance-cn.org/imprimer.html?id_article=16495&lang=fr&cs=print)
with 8 other representatives of Chinese environmental NGOs to [meet
several institutions](http://www.greensos.cn/ContentPage.aspx?xh=97025)
there.  After long years of struggle and fighting, she can now proudly
proclaim:

> *"The authorities have already closed a few factories. In one case
> they even demolished the machines so that they couldn't be used
> anymore."*

<div>

The [Green Law
China](http://www.greenlawchina.org/2009/07/a-note-from-the-province-of-the-nine-headed-bird/)
blog says that  *"People like Yun Jianli are the drivers of positive
change in China."* I totally agree and would like to add that there are
many like her waiting in China to bring new hope to a country that is
now learning to face its problems.
